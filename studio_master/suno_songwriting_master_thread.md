# Suno Songwriting Master Thread

## Purpose
A compact source-of-truth doc for how we should approach hit-oriented songwriting and Suno prompting going forward.

This is not the full research archive. It is the distilled version of what actually matters.

---

## Core thesis
The biggest pattern is simple:

**Great pop writing usually optimizes melody, stress pattern, vowel shape, cadence, and sectional lift before it worries about “beautiful lyrics.”**

The lyric still matters, but at the highest level it usually has to:
- sit cleanly on the beat
- feel natural in the mouth
- support the melody instead of fighting it
- make the chorus land instantly

**Working rule:** write the line that sings best first, then make it mean something.

---

## What matters most

### 1. Melody is the boss
If a line reads well but sings poorly, it is not the right line.

### 2. Syllables must lock
When two phrases play the same melodic or rhythmic role, they usually need matching syllable count and similar stress shape.

If that “math” breaks, the section often starts sounding cheaper or less inevitable.

### 3. Lyrics are partly percussion
A strong pop lyric is not just meaning. It is also mouthfeel, attack, bounce, and vowel shape.

That means:
- open vowels often sing better in big moments
- awkward consonant collisions can kill flow
- nonsense or dummy-vowel drafting can be useful before final wording

### 4. Natural phrasing beats clever phrasing
The line should sound like something a real artist would naturally sing, not like a writer trying to prove they are smart.

### 5. Contrast creates lift
Big songs usually create energy through contrast:
- verse vs chorus
- narrow melody vs wide melody
- restrained phrasing vs open phrasing
- dense rhythm vs simpler, more anthemic payoff

## The main warning
Technical cadence is not enough.

A song can become more “correct” and still get flatter.

When that happens, the fix is usually not more syllable math. The fix is:
- stronger concept
- sharper emotional framing
- more identity in the details
- a better title or hook idea

**Discipline builds the vehicle. It does not supply the soul.**

## Our practical writing framework

### Step 1. Concept sentence
One sentence only.

This should describe the emotional engine of the song, not the plot.

Example:
> Two people trying to act cool while the rhythm exposes them.

### Step 2. Hook phrase
Find a 3 to 6 word phrase that feels good in the mouth and could carry the chorus.

Good hook phrases are usually:
- easy to repeat
- emotionally legible
- vowel-friendly
- rhythmically clean

### Step 3. Dummy-vowel pass
Before forcing perfect lyrics, sketch the melody with loose words, nonsense sounds, or placeholder phrases.

This helps us find:
- real cadence
- natural stresses
- where the melody wants to breathe
- which vowels want to open up

### Step 4. Cadence pass
Check every major line for:
- syllable count
- stress pattern
- vowel openness
- hard consonant collisions
- line length consistency within paired phrases

### Step 5. Meaning pass
Now sharpen the lyric without breaking the cadence.

Only replace words that actually improve the emotional hit.

### Step 6. Section lift pass
Ask:
- does the chorus feel wider, simpler, or more inevitable than the verse?
- is there enough contrast?
- do we preview the hook too early or not enough?
- does the post-chorus or refrain earn its repetition?

## Suno-specific conclusions

### What seems to work best for us
- shorter, cleaner style strings
- clear genre and mood tags
- strong structure cues
- lyrics that are already rhythmically stable
- one generation goal at a time

### What tends to hurt
- giant essay prompts
- too many competing style ideas
- over-written lyrics that read better than they sing
- trying to solve concept, structure, production, and novelty all in one pass

### Working rule for prompting
Use Suno to execute a focused idea, not to discover the entire song from chaos.

That means each generation should have a clear purpose, such as:
- find the right vocal tone
- test a cleaner chorus
- test a genre variant
- test a more anthemic structure

## Our recommended workflow

### Best default workflow
You give me:
- the emotional concept
- 3 reference songs
- whether the vocal should feel male, female, duet, stacked, intimate, huge, etc.
- 5 to 10 style strings or prompts that have already performed well for you

I give you:
- 1 core concept
- 3 title options
- 1 master lyric
- 1 cadence-optimized rewrite
- 3 style prompt variants: short / medium / weird
- a short note on what to test next

This is the best overall process because it balances songwriting craft with actual model behavior.

## What we should optimize for going forward

### Priority order
1. concept
2. hook phrase
3. melody and cadence
4. chorus lift
5. artist-natural phrasing
6. production/style prompt
7. polishing lyric details

If the song is weak at #1 through #4, polishing #7 will not save it.

---

## Fast checklist for future songs
Before we call a lyric draft “good,” check:

- Does the hook feel good in the mouth?
- Can the chorus be understood on first listen?
- Do parallel lines have matching rhythmic logic?
- Are there any awkward clunky consonant pileups?
- Are the vowels too closed for the big notes?
- Does the line sound sung, not just written?
- Does the chorus actually lift?
- Is the concept emotionally clear in one sentence?
- Does the song feel specific without becoming wordy?
- Is the prompt focused enough for Suno to execute cleanly?

---

## The simplest summary
**The line has to sing cleanly, feel natural, and make the chorus arrive fast.**

That is the center of gravity.

Everything else is in service of that.
